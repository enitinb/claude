## Chapter 1 — The scarce resource

> "We talk about attention as if it were a faculty we possess. In an economy organized around capturing it, it is more accurately a substance we lease. Each notification, each scroll session, each tab kept open in case of need — these are not interruptions to our work. They are the terms of our employment in someone else's."

> "The cliché that 'time is money' has been true for a century. The newer claim, that 'attention is money,' is not a metaphor. There exist functioning markets — programmatic ad exchanges, recommendation systems, content marketplaces — in which seconds of attention are priced, sold, and re-sold in literal cents."

## Chapter 4 — The friction question

> "We have spent twenty years removing friction from every interaction. Single sign-on. One-click checkout. Frictionless social. The result is not freedom; the result is a system in which every habit you have ever wanted to break is exactly one tap away from re-engaging."

> "The therapeutic move, in this regime, is to *reintroduce friction* — strategically, even artificially. A locked drawer for the phone. A laptop that lives in a different room. A separate browser, with a separate password, for the work that matters."

> *(Margin note from reader: "this is what I did with email and it worked for two weeks then I just got tired of the friction")*

## Chapter 7 — Whose problem is this?

> "It is fashionable to blame social media for our distractedness, and the case is not without merit. But blaming social media for distraction is like blaming gravity for falling — accurate, perhaps, but not very useful as a guide to action. The deeper question is why our cognitive habits, formed in an era of scarcity of information, have not adapted to its abundance."

> "I will go further. I do not believe social media is the *cause* of the attention crisis. I believe it is the most successful business model that the underlying crisis has produced. The crisis is older. It is the crisis of a species that has not yet built cultural antibodies to its own technologies."

## Chapter 11 — On unstructured time

> "The most countercultural act available to a knowledge worker in 2026 is to be unreachable for two hours."

> "Deep work is overrated as a productivity technique and underrated as a way of being alive. The point of two hours of unbroken attention is not the additional output. The point is the quality of contact between you and the thing you are doing."
