# Highlights — "Against frictionless habits" (blog post)

*From a fictional blog read on 2026-04-19.*

---

> "The wellness-tech industry sells frictionless habits — habits so easy you can't fail at them. Open the app, log the meditation, get the streak. The pitch makes sense for week one. By week six, the question 'is this doing anything?' has been answered by your brain in the negative, and the streak is the only thing left."

> "Real change requires *some* friction, because friction is what makes the change feel like a choice you keep making."

> "I'm increasingly suspicious of any productivity or wellness advice that frames the goal as 'making the right thing easier.' Sometimes the right thing is hard *and that's the point.*"

> "If the frictionless path through your day looks identical to the path you don't want — same phone, same apps, same notifications — you don't have a habits problem. You have an environment problem. Change the environment."

> "We have inherited a default environment designed by people whose business model depends on your attention. Treating that environment as neutral is the first mistake."

>
> > Reply (author): *Not wrong, partial. Reducing friction works for habits you want to start (flossing, taking vitamins). Adding friction works for habits you want to stop (scrolling, snacking). The standard advice flattens the two cases.*

> *"What's your actual setup right now?"*
>
> > Reply (author): *Phone in a drawer when I'm writing. Separate browser profile for work, no social-media bookmarks. Email opens at 10am. A timer in my notes app instead of in my watch app, so checking time doesn't tempt me into messages. None of this was natural; all of it was designed.*
