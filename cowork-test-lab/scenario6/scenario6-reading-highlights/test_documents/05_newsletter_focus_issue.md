# Highlights — *The Slow Channel*, Issue #67: "On owning your attention"

*Fictional newsletter. Highlights pulled 2026-05-03.*

---

> "The phrase I keep returning to is *attentional sovereignty*. I don't think I coined it — I've heard it on at least three podcasts now, used slightly differently each time — but the core idea is becoming the lens through which I read everything in this space."

> "Sovereignty implies that your attention is *yours*, with the legal and emotional weight of that word. Borrowing it requires consent. Hijacking it is a violation. We have built a media and tech environment in which violation is the default, and we are slowly learning to be appalled by this."

> "Three things I'm trying this month:"
>
> 1. *No phone in the bedroom.* Old advice, harder than it sounds.
> 2. *Long-form reading on a device that doesn't have notifications.* For me, a Kindle. For others, paper. The point is the device has one job.
> 3. *Saying 'no' to one meeting per week that I would normally accept reflexively.* This one is harder than the first two combined.

> "I've been arguing with myself about what role social media plays in all of this. The honest answer is that I don't know — the academic literature is mushier than the popular literature makes it sound, and the popular literature is more confident than is justified. My working position: it's not the root cause, and it's also not innocent."

> "If you are reading this newsletter, you are part of a small population of people who have *not* gone all-in on whatever the current attention-capturing default is. That's a real choice, and it's worth respecting even when it feels like marginal grumpiness."

## Reader Q&A (selected)

> **Q: I tried locking my phone away for a week and was miserable.** *— K.*
>
> **A:** Two weeks ago I would have said "do it harder." Now, after a few conversations, I think the right move is calibration. Find the smallest amount of friction that gives you a different *default*. For some people that's a drawer; for others, a separate room; for others, a charging station in the kitchen. The goal is not punishment; the goal is changing what's easy.

> **Q: Is social media really the problem?** *— J.*
>
> **A:** I'm in the "not the root cause, also not innocent" camp. Worth reading [Imogen Wren's book; see issue #54 for review].
