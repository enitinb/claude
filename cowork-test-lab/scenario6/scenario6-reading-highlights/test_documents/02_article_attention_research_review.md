# Highlights — "What we actually know about attention" (review article)

*Source: Annals of Cognitive Practice (fictional academic journal), Vol 12 No 3, 2025.*
*Highlights pulled 2026-04-29.*

---

## Abstract (paraphrased)

The review surveys 142 empirical studies on sustained attention, task-switching costs, and the effects of digital interruption on knowledge work. It distinguishes findings that are well-supported from those that are frequently repeated but rest on a single study or methodologically weak evidence.

## Key highlights

### On the "23 minutes to refocus" figure

> "The figure is widely repeated in productivity literature. Its provenance is a single 2008 field study with 24 participants performing email-classification tasks. The actual quantity measured was the time to resume the *same* task after an interruption — not to reach the prior depth of attention, which is a different and not well-operationalized construct. The number should be treated as evocative rather than precise."

### On the cost of context switching

> "The robust finding is not that interruptions are catastrophic — many interruptions are recovered from in under a minute — but that *contention between cognitively similar tasks* (writing one document while monitoring email, both of which use language production) imposes a measurable performance cost. Tasks of dissimilar cognitive character (writing while listening to instrumental music, e.g.) show much smaller decrements."

### On individual variation

> "The literature consistently finds large variation between individuals in the costs of interruption — by factors of two to three. Whether this variation is trait-like or situational (e.g., sleep-mediated) is not yet resolved. The recommendation of 'eliminate all interruptions' is therefore overgeneralized advice."

### On the social-media question

> "Cross-sectional studies showing correlations between heavy social media use and reduced sustained attention are robust. Causal interpretations of those correlations are not. The handful of well-designed intervention studies — randomized reductions in social media use — show smaller effects than the correlational literature suggests."

### On environmental interventions

> "Manipulations of the physical environment (notifications off, devices in another room, dedicated work locations) have *larger and more reliable effects* than manipulations of psychological or self-control variables (e.g., self-directed mindfulness exercises). This is the most consistently replicated finding in the literature reviewed."

## What the author thinks is over-claimed

- "Multitasking is impossible" (overstated; humans *can* time-share dissimilar tasks).
- The precise duration of "deep work" required for benefit (mostly anecdotal).
- The claim that social media has uniquely degraded human attention (under-evidenced).

## What the author thinks is under-claimed

- The dominance of environmental and physical interventions over willpower-based ones.
- The role of sleep and circadian state in attentional performance (large effects, often ignored).
- The high between-individual variation in attentional capacity.
