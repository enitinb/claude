## Quick summary

Tomas interviews Imogen about her book, with most of the conversation focused on her practical recommendations and the pushback she's gotten from her own readers.

## Best moments

**On the friction argument:**

> Imogen: "The most common email I get is from someone saying they tried locking their phone in a drawer for three days and it didn't stick. My response is — of course it didn't. Friction has to be calibrated. Too much friction and you abandon the change. Too little and the friction itself becomes invisible. The art is finding the precise amount of inconvenience that you can sustain."

**On the limits of her own framing:**

> Imogen: "I want to be honest about something the book maybe undersells. I made the case that social media is downstream of a deeper attentional crisis. After spending another year talking to people about this, I think both are true. The crisis is older than the platforms, yes. But the platforms are extraordinarily good at exploiting it. Diagnosing it as upstream lets the platforms off too easy. So I've updated."

**On Tomas's "attentional sovereignty" framing:**

> Tomas: "I keep using the phrase 'attentional sovereignty' on this show — the idea that your attention belongs to you and the world has to ask before it borrows. Does that resonate with what you wrote?"
>
> Imogen: "Yes, and I wish I'd had the phrase when I was writing. That's the substance of chapter 11. The book is about negotiating the terms of a transaction that, until you name it, doesn't seem like a transaction."

**On individual variation:**

> Imogen: "The other thing I'd revise — I wrote the book like everyone's attentional system was roughly the same. I now think that's wrong by a lot. Some people genuinely cannot work with music, some can't work without it, some recover from interruption in 30 seconds, some need 30 minutes. The advice has to be calibrated to your own pattern."

## Practical advice from the episode

1. *Run the experiment on yourself.* Don't trust generic advice. Track for two weeks what conditions produce your good work and what produces your bad work.
2. *Calibrate friction.* If a change doesn't stick, the friction is wrong (too much or too little), not your willpower.
3. *Protect the first two hours.* Imogen specifically: "If you do nothing else, do not open communication tools in the first two hours of the day."
4. *Sleep is the upstream lever.* Tomas brought this up; Imogen agreed it should have been a chapter in the book.
